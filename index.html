Chapter 01

Basic Concepts and Game Rule Definitions

What is the Min-Knapsack Problem?

The classic offline minimization knapsack problem is defined as follows: given a knapsack with a capacity of \(1\), and a series of items with different sizes and costs, our goal is to select a set of items such that their total size at least covers the knapsack capacity (i.e., total size \(\ge 1\)), while keeping the total cost as small as possible.

🔒 Click to expand: Rigorous Mathematical Definition ▶

Input: A set of items \(D = \{d_1, \ldots, d_n\}\). Each item \(d_i\) has cost \(c(d_i) \in \mathbb{R}^+\) and size \(s(d_i) \in \mathbb{R}^+\).

Output: Subset \(F \subseteq D\) minimizing \(\sum_{f \in F} c(f)\), subject to \(\sum_{f \in F} s(f) \ge 1\).

Online Model

In the "online" model, items arrive one by one over time. The algorithm must make an immediate decision to accept or reject when the current item arrives, before seeing the next item. We cannot predict when the input sequence will end, so the algorithm faces immense uncertainty.

Competitive Analysis (Competitive Ratio)

Have you ever thought about how we measure whether our choices are good or not when we cannot predict the future? A very natural idea is to compare it with a "God's-eye view"—that is, comparing it with what we would do if we knew what the sequence looked like. In algorithms, this is called the Competitive Ratio.

🔒 Click to expand: Mathematical Definition of Competitive Ratio ▶

Let \(A(L)\) be the total cost of the online algorithm when processing input sequence \(L\), and \(\text{OPT}(L)\) be the cost of the optimal offline algorithm. The competitive ratio \(R_A\) is:

Definition
\[R_A = \sup_L \frac{A(L)}{\text{OPT}(L)}\]
Chapter 02

The Foundation for Breaking Through: Two "Overlord Clauses" of Online Algorithms

Since we have to survive in such a treacherous environment, what strategy should we adopt? Mathematicians have proven that if an online algorithm is to have a controllable competitive ratio, it must satisfy the following two seemingly extreme necessary conditions:

Condition 1: The "Beggars Can't Be Choosers" Principle (Accept while size < 1)

Rule: As long as the total survival points in your knapsack have not reached 100 points (total size \(< 1\)), you must unconditionally accept the new incoming item!

Reader's Doubt

"Wait, isn't this too stupid? If the first thing the boss takes out is a piece of trash priced at $10,000, do I have to pinch my nose and buy it?"

⚡ The Truth

Yes, you must buy it. Because in the online world, you are facing an extremely malicious "boss (Adversary)." If you reject this trash to wait for better equipment, the boss might immediately close up shop (stop the input). At this point, your knapsack is not full, and you starve to death in the wilderness (algorithm cost is infinite), whereas the optimal solution (OPT) with the "God's-eye view" could have completely bought this trash and survived. This causes your competitive ratio to explode directly.

Condition 2: The "Seven-Day No-Reason Return" Principle (Removable Condition)

Since condition 1 will force us to buy sky-high priced trash, how can we save ourselves? The answer is: the algorithm must be allowed to discard (return) items already placed in the knapsack!

Suppose the first item the boss gives you has a huge size (let it be \(M\)) and an extremely high cost (let it be \(M\)). To guard against the boss closing up shop, you are forced to buy it. But immediately after, the boss takes out a perfect item with a size of \(1\) and a cost of only \(1\).

No Returns

Disaster

Your knapsack is firmly stuck with that sky-high priced trash, and your cost is \(M\). Meanwhile, OPT knows there is a perfect item coming later, so its cost is \(1\). As \(M \to \infty\), your performance will be incredibly terrible.

Returns Allowed

Recovery

You can decisively throw away the sky-high priced trash and replace it with the perfect item! This "return" mechanism is the key to controlling costs.

Why Will Traditional Greedy Algorithms Fail?

With the privilege of "returns and exchanges," you might think: "Then shouldn't I just pick items according to their 'unit cost' (cost/size) from smallest to largest? Whichever is the cheapest per survival point, I keep."

Sounds perfect? Please do an experiment yourself in the simulator below. In this simulator, the system forces you to follow the Greedy Algorithm logic: you must prioritize items with the lowest cost-per-point and discard anything redundant to save money. See what kind of Waterloo you encounter.

Item Cost Size Unit Cost Your Decision Greedy Says

As you might have discovered, traditional greedy algorithms have an unbounded competitive ratio in online scenarios. This means a malicious boss can make you spend infinitely more than you should.

Here is the "Greedy Trap" proof from the paper:

  1. Item 1 arrives: Cost = \(1\), Size = \(1\). (Unit cost = \(1\))
  2. Item 2 arrives: Cost = \(2\epsilon\), Size = \(\epsilon\). (Unit cost = \(2\)) — The Greedy algorithm compares the unit costs. Since Item 1 (\(1\)) is cheaper than Item 2 (\(2\)), it keeps Item 1 and rejects Item 2 to avoid redundant spending.
  3. Item 3 arrives: Cost = \(2\epsilon\), Size = \(1-\epsilon\). (Unit cost \(\approx 2\epsilon\)) — To reach the total size of \(1\), Greedy now picks Item 3 to combine with Item 1.
Greedy Cost

\(1 + 2\epsilon\)

As \(\epsilon \to 0\), this approaches $1. Stuck with the expensive Item 1.

OPT (God's View)

\(4\epsilon\)

Keep Item 2 + Item 3. As \(\epsilon \to 0\), cost approaches $0.

💥 Conclusion

The ratio \(\frac{1+2\epsilon}{4\epsilon} \to \infty\) as \(\epsilon \to 0\). The greedy algorithm has an unbounded competitive ratio. Since local greed is a dead end, we urgently need a strategy with a "big picture view." Next, we officially enter the world of Algorithm A.

Chapter 04

Algorithm A's Design Intuition: From the "Greedy Trap" to a "Dimensionality Reduction Strike"

The simulator just now revealed to us the fundamental reason for the failure of the greedy algorithm: it attempts to use a single metric (cost-effectiveness) across huge size differences to make irreversible global comparisons.

Taking a small screw that only provides 1 survival point and hard-comparing its unit price with a heavy tent that provides 90 survival points is obviously unreasonable. To precisely patch the loophole of the greedy algorithm, the design of Algorithm A is based on the following progressive core intuitions:

Intuition 1: Refuse Cross-Boundary Comparisons (Exponential Grouping)

Thought: "Since items of vastly different sizes are hard to compare fairly, let's force them into 'weight classes'."

Dimensionality Reduction Method: Algorithm A divides items into groups based on their size. Each group (bin) \(S_j\) holds items whose size falls between \(2^j\) and \(2^{j+1}\). This limits the size difference within any single bin to a factor of at most 2.

Bin Assignment Rule
\[S_j = \{ d \in D \mid 2^j < s(d) \le 2^{j+1} \}\]

Intuition 2: Same-Class Competition (Lowest Cost First)

Thought: "Inside a bin, everyone is roughly the same size. If sizes are similar, the problem becomes simple: who's the cheapest?"

Dimensionality Reduction Method: Within each "weight class," we stop worrying about complex cost-effectiveness ratios. We simply prioritize the items with the lowest absolute cost.

Intuition 3: The "Memoryless" Dynamic Anchor (Update β & The 3× Rule)

Grouping helps us choose, but how do we know if our entire backpack is getting too expensive?

Thought: "We need a standard to decide when to stop buying or start returning. But there's a catch: we can only use the items we currently have. We've forgotten everything we already threw away."

Dimensionality Reduction Method: At each step \(t\), the algorithm looks at the items in your bag plus the new arrival (\(E(t)\)) and guesses the best possible cost — this is our valuation anchor \(\beta(t)\).

⚔️ The 3× Purge Rule

The 3× Purge: To keep costs under control, we monitor each bin. If a bin's total cost exceeds \(3\beta(t)\), we repeatedly kick out the most expensive item in that bin until the cost drops back below the line.

Chapter 06

Performance Analysis of Algorithm A: Why is it O(log Δ)?

After the previous "grouping" and "elimination," how much money can our Algorithm A actually save? In computer science, we cannot just rely on feelings to say "it's good"; we need an absolute mathematical promise.

If you feel dizzy seeing big O notation like \(O(\log \Delta)\), don't be afraid. Let's first use a "bill" to explain the intuition behind it.

📊 Intuitive Explanation: Settling the Total Bill

Before the heavy math, let's look at the "Final Bill" logic:

  1. Max bill per bin: The "3× Purge" rule ensures no bin exceeds \(3\beta(t)\).
  2. Total bins: Bins are divided exponentially, so we only need \(\lceil \log_2 \Delta \rceil + 1\) bins to cover all item sizes.
  3. Upper Bound: Total cost \(\le 3\beta(t) \times (\lceil \log_2 \Delta \rceil + 1)\).
Total Cost Upper Bound
\[A(D(t)) \le 3\beta(t) \times (\lceil \log_2 \Delta \rceil + 1)\]

Because \(\beta(t)\) converges to a constant multiple of OPT, stripping the constant gives competitive ratio \(O(\log \Delta)\).

🛡️ Hardcore Derivation: The Complete Logical Chain (Han & Makino, 2016)

For the "math geeks" who want to see how the roof stays on the house, here is the full sequence of lemmas as presented in the original paper.

🔒 Click to expand: Full Mathematical Proofs (Lemmas 3–6) ▶

Lemma 3. Let \(j\) be an integer. At time \(t \ge t_0\), we have \(c(p) \ge c(q)\) for all \(p \in D_j(t) \setminus F_j(t)\) and \(q \in F_j(t)\).

Proof. For a fixed \(j\), we prove this lemma by induction. The lemma holds for time \(t = t_0\) because there are no \(p \in D_j(t-1) \setminus F_j(t-1)\) and the highest cost items are removed first.

Assume that the lemma holds for time \(t_1 \ge t_0\) and consider time \(t = t_1 + 1\). If \(d_t \notin F_j(t)\), then the inductive hypothesis implies that the lemma holds, since Step 3 always removes the item with the highest cost. If \(d_t \in F_j(t)\), let \(\gamma\) be the cost of the cheapest item in \(D_j(t) \setminus F_j(t)\). We show that \(c(d_t) \le \gamma\), which together with the inductive hypothesis implies that the lemma is true for this case.

Let \(t'\ (< t)\) be the maximum time when an item with cost \(\gamma\) is removed or rejected. During \((t', t]\), no items in \(F_j(t')\) are removed otherwise there is a later time when an item with cost \(\gamma\) is removed or there is a lower cost item in \(D_j(t) \setminus F_j(t)\). So, we have \(F_j(t') \subseteq F_j(t)\).

If \(c(d_t) > \gamma\), then by the monotonicity of \(\beta\), we have

\[ c(F_j(t)) \ge c(F_j(t')) + \gamma > 3\beta(t') \ge 3\beta(t), \]

which is a contradiction. ∎

Observation 1. If there is a feasible solution in \(E(t)\), then \(\beta(t) \ge \text{OPT}(D(t))\).

Lemma 4. For time \(t \ge t_0\), assume there is a feasible solution in \(F(t)\), i.e., \(s(F(t)) \ge 1\). Then, for all \(j\) such that \(F_j^*(t) \not\subseteq F_j(t)\), we have \(c(F_j(t)) \ge 2\beta(t)\).

Proof. Assume that \(F_j^*(t) \not\subseteq F_j(t)\) for some \(j\) and \(t \ge t_0\). Let \(\gamma\) be the cost of a cheapest item in \(D_j(t) - F_j(t)\), and let \(t'\ (\le t)\) be the maximum time when an item with cost \(\gamma\) is discarded. As mentioned in the proof of Lemma 3, we have \(F_j(t') \subseteq F_j(t)\). This implies \(c(F_j(t')) \le c(F_j(t))\). Let \(x \in F_j^*(t) \setminus F_j(t)\). By the definition of \(\gamma\), we have \(c(x) \ge \gamma\). Hence,

\[ \gamma \le c(F_j^*(t)) \le \text{OPT}(D(t)) \le \beta(t), \]

where the last inequality holds from Observation 1. By Step 3 of the algorithm, \(c(F_j(t')) \ge 3\beta(t') - \gamma \ge 3\beta(t') - \beta(t) \ge 2\beta(t)\), and hence \(c(F_j(t)) \ge 2\beta(t)\). ∎

Lemma 5. At any time \(t \ge t_0\), \(F(t)\) contains a feasible solution for \(D(t)\) with cost at most \(2\,\text{OPT}(D(t))\).

Proof. Recall that \(F^*(t)\) denotes an optimal solution for an input \(D(t)\) and \(F_j^*(t) = F^*(t) \cap S_j\). For each \(j\), we show that there exists \(G_j \subseteq F_j(t)\) such that

\[ s(G_j) \ge s(F_j^*(t)) \quad \text{and} \quad c(G_j) \le 2c(F_j^*(t)). \]

This completes the proof, since \(\bigcup_j G_j\) is a feasible solution for \(D(t)\) with cost at most \(2\,\text{OPT}(D(t))\).

We construct \(G_j\) as follows. If \(F_j^*(t) \subseteq F_j(t)\), then \(G_j := F_j^*(t)\). Otherwise, we construct \(G_j\) from \(F_j^*(t) \cap F_j(t)\) by adding the cheapest \(2|F_j^*(t) \setminus F_j(t)|\) items from \(F_j(t) \setminus F_j^*(t)\).

By Lemma 3, we have \(c(p) \ge c(q)\) holds for all \(p \in F_j^*(t) \setminus F_j(t)\) and \(q \in F_j(t)\). Thus \(c(G_j) \le 2c(F_j^*(t))\). By the definition of \(S_j\), we have \(s(p)/2 \le s(q)\) for any items \(p\) and \(q\) in \(S_j\), which implies \(s(G_j) \ge s(F_j^*(t))\).

We prove \(|F_j(t) \setminus F_j^*(t)| \ge 2|F_j^*(t) \setminus F_j(t)|\) by induction on \(t \ge t_0\). At time \(t = t_0\), we have \(\beta(t) \ge \text{OPT}(D(t))\). If \(F_j^*(t) \not\subseteq F_j(t)\), by Lemma 4:

\[ c(F_j(t)) \ge 2\beta(t) \ge 2\,\text{OPT}(D(t)) \ge 2c(F_j^*(t)), \]

and hence \(c(F_j(t) \setminus F_j^*(t)) \ge 2c(F_j^*(t) \setminus F_j(t))\). By Lemma 3, \(c(p) \le c(q)\) for any two items \(p \in F_j(t) \setminus F_j^*(t)\) and \(q \in F_j^*(t) \setminus F_j(t)\). Hence \(|F_j(t) \setminus F_j^*(t)| \ge 2|F_j^*(t) \setminus F_j(t)|\) for \(t = t_0\). The inductive step follows by the same argument. ∎

Lemma 6. Algorithm A is \(O(\log \Delta)\)-competitive.

Proof. By Lemma 5, \(F(t)\) contains a feasible solution for \(D(t)\) with cost at most \(2\,\text{OPT}(D(t))\). Since (offline) Min-Knapsack admits an FPTAS [1] and \(F(t) \subseteq E(t)\) from Step 2 of the algorithm,

\[ \beta(t) \le (1+\epsilon)\,\text{OPT}(E(t)) \le (1+\epsilon)\,\text{OPT}(F(t)) \le 2(1+\epsilon)\,\text{OPT}(D(t)) \]

for some \(\epsilon > 0\), and the cost by Algorithm A satisfies

\[ A(D(t)) \le 3(\lceil \log_2 \Delta \rceil + 1)\,\beta(t), \]

and hence \(A(D(t)) \le O(\log \Delta)\,\text{OPT}(D(t))\). ∎

Lemma 7. Algorithm A is \(\Omega(\log \Delta)\)-competitive.

Proof. To prove this lemma, we present an instance \(D\) such that \(A(D) \ge \log \Delta \cdot \text{OPT}(D)\).

For \(0 \le i \le k\), let \(b_i\) be an item with \(s(b_i) = c(b_i) = 2^{-i}\), and we construct an input sequence \(D\) by \(D = D^{(0)}, D^{(1)}, \ldots, D^{(k)}\), where \(D^{(i)}\) is a sequence consisting of \(2^i\) copies of \(b_i\). Note that this instance has an optimal solution \(F^* = \{b_0\}\) whose cost is \(\text{OPT}(D) = 1\). On the other hand, Algorithm A keeps all the items, and hence \(A(D) = k+1 > \log_2 \Delta \cdot \text{OPT}(D)\), where \(\Delta = 2^k\) is the ratio of the largest size to the smallest size. ∎

Theorem 1. Algorithm A is \(\Theta(\log \Delta)\)-competitive.

Follows directly from Lemmas 6 and 7. ∎
